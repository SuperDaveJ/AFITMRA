While there are many benefits to the Manufacturing Readiness Assessment, there are some potential drawbacks that you need to keep in mind. Mainly, it is difficult to get a Manufacturing Readiness Assessment into a current contract. There are several reasons for this.

  • One: A Manufacturing Readiness Assessment is perceived as using already limited resources and unnecessary time. People don’t know where or how to get the resources (that is, find Subject Matter Experts and people with the proper expertise). In addition, most people feel like they are already undermanned and finding those resources looks like an additional burden.
  • Two: People may not want to find out issues with their program that are currently unknown. They don’t want to discover new or hidden issues.
  • Three: The question always comes up, where are the funds coming from to fix those problems? Along those same lines of thought, there is a resistance to Manufacturing Readiness Assessments because it may not produce an immediate gain for the cost.

Are there ways to minimize the drawbacks or the extent that they proliferate? One way to do that is to know and have a good relationship with your Procuring Contracting Officer. He or she is a good source for Subject Matter Experts and people with the proper expertise, as well as a good resource for funding issues.
Another way to minimize the drawbacks is to build a good, well-rounded team for the MRA.

Remember, even though there are drawbacks to the Manufacturing Readiness Assessment process, the value of conducting the Manufacturing Readiness Assessment far outweighs those drawbacks.
